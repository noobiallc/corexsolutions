Google & Visibility

How to Get More Calls from Google
Without Running Ads

By Cris L., CoreX Solutions
April 23, 2026
6 min read

There's a common misconception among service business owners that showing up on Google requires paying Google. That if you want calls from search, you need to run ads.

That's not true — and for local service businesses especially, it's an expensive belief to hold.

Google actually provides a set of free tools specifically designed to help local businesses show up when nearby customers are searching. Most businesses either don't know they exist, or they set them up once and never touched them again. Either way, they're leaving a significant amount of free visibility on the table.

This article covers the organic side of Google — the results that show up because of how your business is set up and presented, not because you paid for placement. Here's how to make the most of it.

"The businesses that show up at the top of Google for 'plumber near me' or 'roofer Gwinnett County' aren't necessarily spending more on ads. They've just done a better job telling Google who they are."

Start Here: Claim and Complete Your Google Business Profile

Your Google Business Profile (formerly called Google My Business) is the single most important free tool Google offers local businesses. It's the listing that shows up in the map pack — those three business results that appear above the regular search results when someone searches for a local service.

That map pack gets a disproportionate share of clicks. People searching for a contractor or service provider often never scroll past it. If your business isn't in it, you're invisible to a significant portion of potential customers.

Here's the problem: many service businesses either haven't claimed their profile at all, or they claimed it years ago with incomplete information and never updated it. Google uses that information to decide which businesses to show — and how high to rank them.

If you haven't done this yet, search your business name on Google and look for the option to "claim this business." If your profile doesn't exist at all, you can create one at business.google.com.

The Five Things That Actually Move Your Ranking

Once you have your profile, these are the elements Google weighs most heavily when deciding whether to show your business in the local map pack:

  • 01
    NAP Consistency — Name, Address, Phone

    Your business name, address, and phone number need to be identical everywhere they appear online — your Google profile, your website, Yelp, Angi, Facebook, and any other directory. Even small differences (like "Street" vs "St") can hurt your ranking. Google uses these signals to verify that your business is real and located where you say it is.

  • 02
    Reviews — Volume, Recency, and Your Responses

    Reviews are one of the most powerful ranking factors for local search. Not just the star rating — Google looks at how many reviews you have, how recently you've been getting them, and whether you respond to them. A business with 12 reviews and no responses ranks below one with 80 recent reviews and active engagement.

  • 03
    Category and Service Accuracy

    Your primary business category in Google should be as specific as possible. "HVAC contractor" outperforms "contractor" for HVAC searches. And your services list — which many businesses leave blank — tells Google exactly what searches you should show up for. Fill it out completely, using the language your customers would use.

  • 04
    Photos — Real, Recent, and Relevant

    Profiles with photos get significantly more clicks than those without. Stock photos don't help — Google and customers both respond better to real images of your work, your team, and your vehicles. Post new photos regularly; profiles that are actively updated signal to Google that the business is operating.

  • 05
    Google Posts — Using the Built-In Content Tool

    Most businesses never use Google Posts, which is a free feature that lets you publish updates, offers, and announcements directly to your Business Profile. These posts appear in your listing and show that the business is active. They also give you an additional way to include the specific services and keywords you want to be found for.

Quick audit: Search your own business on Google. Does the information match your website exactly? Are there more than 20 reviews? Is there a photo posted in the last 90 days? These three checks alone will tell you where your biggest gap is.

Getting More Reviews Without Awkward Conversations

For most service businesses, reviews are the biggest gap — and also the highest-leverage thing to fix. The businesses that rank in the top three in a market almost always have significantly more reviews than the ones that don't appear at all.

The most effective way to get more reviews isn't complicated: ask for them, at the right moment, with as little friction as possible.

The right moment is immediately after you finish a job — while the customer's satisfaction is fresh. You can say this in person: "If you were happy with the work, a quick Google review would really help us out." Then send a text within a few hours with a direct link to your Google review page. The fewer steps between the customer's intent and the review, the more reviews you'll actually get.

What most businesses do instead: they hope customers will leave reviews on their own. Some do. Most don't — not because they were unhappy, but because the process takes too much initiative. Removing that friction is the entire game.

"Your happiest customers want to support you. They just need to be asked, and they need it to be easy."

Your Website and Google Work Together

Your Google Business Profile and your website are not separate things — Google looks at both together to decide how to rank you.

Specifically, Google checks whether the information on your website matches your profile, whether your site mentions the service areas you claim to cover, and whether your site has the kind of content that signals expertise in what you do.

This means a few practical things for your website:

  • Your phone number, address, and business name should appear on your website exactly as they do on your Google profile
  • Your homepage and service pages should explicitly mention the cities and areas you serve — not just by listing them, but by using natural language that a customer would use when searching
  • If you serve multiple areas, consider having individual service pages for your most important locations — not duplicate content, but pages with genuinely relevant local information

None of this requires an SEO agency or a technical background. It's mostly a matter of being specific where you've been vague, and consistent where you've been inconsistent.

The Compound Effect of Doing This Right

Here's what makes local SEO different from most marketing: it compounds. Every review you get makes the next review slightly more likely. Every month your profile stays active and updated is another month Google has more data to rank you higher. Every consistent mention of your business name, address, and phone number across the web reinforces the signal that you're a legitimate, established local business.

Ads stop the moment you stop paying. A well-maintained local presence keeps working in the background indefinitely. It's slower to build — but once it's there, it's an asset that generates calls every month without ongoing spend.

The businesses that dominate local search in their market didn't get there overnight. But most of them also didn't do anything exotic. They claimed their profile, got consistent reviews, kept their information accurate, and showed up regularly. That's the whole playbook.

Where to Start Today

If you're going to do one thing after reading this, make it this: search your business name on Google, click through to your Business Profile, and look at it critically.

Is every field filled out? Is your phone number tappable? Are there recent photos? Do the reviews reflect the quality of your work? Is your website address listed and correct?

If any of those are missing or out of date, fix them today. It takes less than an hour, it costs nothing, and it's one of the highest-return things you can do for your business's visibility.

After that, set a reminder to come back every month and post an update, add a photo, and make sure everything is still accurate. That consistency, over time, is what separates the businesses that show up from the ones that don't.
